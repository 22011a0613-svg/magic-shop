The hands behind the magic

Meet the Artisans

These are not just craftspeople — they are the living memory of India's most precious traditions.

🧑‍🎨
Pochampally Ikat
40+ years
Rajamma Goud
Pochampally, Nalgonda
Rajamma began weaving at age 12, sitting beside her mother on a pit loom. Today she creates double ikat sarees whose patterns take three days just to plan — each thread dyed individually before a single pass of the shuttle.
Double Ikat Weaving A technique so complex it exists in only 3 places worldwide
🎨
Nirmal Paintings
30+ years
Venkat Rao
Nirmal, Adilabad
Venkat learned the Nirmal technique from his father at age 10. Each painting begins with a base of cow dung and tamarind seed paste — a preparation unchanged since the 16th century. He is one of fewer than 40 masters still practicing.
Nirmal Lacquerware Painting A GI-tagged craft with 500 years of unbroken tradition
🏺
Bidriware
25+ years
Mohammed Iqbal
Bidar, Karnataka
Mohammed inlays pure silver into blackened zinc-copper alloy entirely by hand — a technique originating in the Bahmani Sultanate of the 14th century. His grandfather taught him; he now teaches his son.
Bidri Silver Inlay Named after Bidar — a 700-year-old royal craft tradition
🪆
Kondapalli Toys
35+ years
Suresh Kumar
Kondapalli, Krishna
Suresh carves from the soft Tella Poniki wood that grows only in local forests. His toys — painted with natural dyes — have been exported to museums in Europe and gifted to foreign dignitaries by the Indian government.
Kondapalli Bommallu 400-year-old wooden toy tradition, GI tagged in 2005
🧣
Gadwal Weave
20+ years
Laxmi Devi
Gadwal, Jogulamba
Laxmi weaves the iconic Gadwal saree — cotton body, pure silk border — on a traditional frame loom. The interlocking joint between cotton and silk is done entirely by hand and is the hallmark of an authentic Gadwal piece.
Gadwal Handloom Sarees A weave so distinctive it has its own GI tag since 2010
📜
Cherial Scrolls
28+ years
Rakesh Padmaraju
Cherial, Warangal
Rakesh paints narrative scrolls used by travelling storytellers called Kathakas. A single scroll can be 40 feet long, depicting an entire epic. He uses only natural pigments — red from stone, green from plants, black from lamp soot.
Cherial Scroll Painting A living oral-visual storytelling tradition of Telangana
